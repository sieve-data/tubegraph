From: [[blueprint.financial]] <br/> 

Retiring abroad can significantly impact Canadian government income benefits like the Canada Pension Plan (CPP), Old Age Security (OAS), and Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:21">[00:00:21]</a>. Understanding how these benefits behave when you leave Canada is crucial to avoid unexpected financial consequences <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:23">[00:00:23]</a>.

## Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS)

The Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS) is a monthly benefit designed to support low-income seniors who live in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:54">[00:00:54]</a>.

### Portability and Residency Requirements
The GIS is **not portable** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:51">[00:00:51]</a>. If you leave Canada for more than six consecutive months, your GIS payments will stop <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:00">[00:01:00]</a>. This rule applies even if your income remains low and you continue filing Canadian taxes <a class="yt-timestamp" data_t="00:01:04">[00:01:04]</a>. The GIS is strictly tied to your physical presence in Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:41">[00:01:41]</a>, and there is no social security agreement that protects it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:37">[00:01:37]</a>.

> [!EXAMPLE] Gan's Experience
> Gan, a 71-year-old retiree receiving CPP and GIS in Vancouver, moved to Mexico <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:10">[00:01:10]</a>. Her GIS was terminated a few months into her move because she was outside Canada for more than six months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:18">[00:01:18]</a>.

If GIS is a critical part of your [[retirement_income_and_financial_security_in_canada | retirement income]], consider its loss before [[effects_of_relocating_on_canadian_retirement_savings_and_benefits | relocating]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:53">[00:01:53]</a>. You may need to delay your move, adjust your budget, or explore other income options <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>.

> [!TIP] Residency Trick (Snowbird Move)
> Some retirees maintain part-year residency in Canada to keep their healthcare and tax status <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:05">[00:02:05]</a>. This involves spending winters in warmer climates but ensuring not to stay outside Canada for more than 183 days in a calendar year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:11">[00:02:11]</a>.

## Canada Pension Plan (CPP)

The Canada Pension Plan (CPP) benefits are based on your contributions during your working life in Canada, not on your residency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:29">[00:02:29]</a>. This means your CPP benefits will travel with you, and the amount received remains the same whether living in Canada or abroad <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:37">[00:02:37]</a>.

> [!EXAMPLE] Linda's Retirement
> Linda worked in Toronto for 35 years, contributing to CPP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:49">[00:02:49]</a>. Even though she plans to retire in Spain, her CPP payments will not change as they are based on her contributions, not her residency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:54">[00:02:54]</a>.

### Tax Considerations for Non-Residents
Your CPP payments may be subject to withholding tax if you are considered a non-resident of Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:14">[00:03:14]</a>.

*   **Tax Treaties**: If Canada has a tax treaty with your new country of residence, this tax could be reduced or even eliminated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:21">[00:03:21]</a>. For example, moving to the US may allow you to avoid withholding tax entirely due to a tax treaty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:32">[00:03:32]</a>.
*   **No Tax Treaty**: Without a tax treaty, you could face a 25% withholding tax on your CPP payments <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:25">[00:03:25]</a>.
*   It is crucial to review your destination's tax treaty with Canada carefully, as these agreements vary widely and can significantly impact the net amount of your CPP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:40">[00:03:40]</a>.

### Social Security Agreements
Canada has social security agreements with over 50 countries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:51">[00:03:51]</a>. These agreements help avoid double contributions, ensure your CPP coverage continues uninterrupted, and coordinate benefits if you have worked in both Canada and abroad <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:57">[00:03:57]</a>.

> [!NOTE] Other CPP Benefits
> CPP survivor benefits and child benefits also continue while living outside Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:07">[00:04:07]</a>.

### Payment Methods
You can have CPP payments direct deposited into a Canadian bank account, or in many countries, receive them in local currency through a local bank <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:16">[00:04:16]</a>. Checks in Canadian dollars can also be mailed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:25">[00:04:25]</a>. While the CPP amount doesn't change, your new country might tax these payments differently, so it's wise to check local tax laws <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:29">[00:04:29]</a>.

## Old Age Security (OAS)

Old Age Security (OAS) benefits are based on residency, not work history or contributions like CPP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:45">[00:04:45]</a>.

### Qualification Requirements for Abroad
To qualify for OAS while living outside Canada, you must have lived in Canada for at least 20 years after turning 18 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:51">[00:04:51]</a>. If you meet this 20-year threshold, your monthly OAS payment will continue, even if you are living permanently overseas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:04">[00:05:04]</a>.

> [!EXAMPLE] Pedro's OAS
> Pedro moved to Canada at age 25 and lived there for 30 years before retiring to Spain <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:10">[00:05:10]</a>. Because he met the 20-year residency requirement, he continues to receive his full OAS payments while living abroad <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:18">[00:05:18]</a>.

### Consequences of Not Meeting the 20-Year Rule
If you have lived in Canada for less than 20 years and leave the country for more than six consecutive months after the month of departure, your OAS will be suspended <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:31">[00:05:31]</a>. You would need to return to Canada and re-establish residency to resume receiving it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:45">[00:05:45]</a>.

### Exceptions
You might still qualify for OAS abroad if you lived or worked in a country that has a social security agreement with Canada, and the combined time you lived in both countries is at least 20 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:50">[00:05:50]</a>. However, the years lived in another country do not increase your OAS amount; they only help determine if you qualify to keep receiving it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:04">[00:06:04]</a>.

### Tax Implications
Your OAS payments are considered taxable income, even when you live abroad <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:12">[00:06:12]</a>.

*   **Withholding Tax**: If you live outside Canada, your OAS may be subject to a non-resident withholding tax <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:28">[00:06:28]</a>. The default rate is 25% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:33">[00:06:33]</a>.
*   **Tax Treaties**: If you live in a country with a tax treaty with Canada, that rate may be reduced or eliminated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:36">[00:06:36]</a>. For example, moving to the US means your OAS is not subject to withholding tax <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:42">[00:06:42]</a>. However, with Germany, Canada does have a tax treaty, but it may not eliminate withholding tax on OAS; instead, it could be taxed at a rate not exceeding 15% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:49">[00:06:49]</a>.
*   You can request voluntary tax deductions by logging into your My Service Canada account or filling out the relevant form <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:21">[00:06:21]</a>.
*   You will receive an NR4 tax slip each year instead of a T4, which you must include when filing your annual tax return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:12">[00:07:12]</a>. You must continue filing a Canadian tax return every year to keep receiving your OAS while abroad <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:22">[00:07:22]</a>.

> [!TIP] Avoiding OAS Clawback
> If you are concerned about hitting the income threshold for the OAS recovery tax (clawback), which begins at $93,454 in 2025, consider managing your taxable income through RRSP withdrawals, pension income splitting, or deferring OAS to age 70 for a higher benefit <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:29">[00:07:29]</a>.

## Planning Strategies & Key Takeaways

Understanding the specific rules for each benefit is essential for successful [[benefits_and_drawbacks_of_retiring_abroad_for_canadians | retirement abroad]].

> [!EXAMPLE] Anita's Retirement Plan
> Anita, 67, is retiring to Portugal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:54">[00:07:54]</a>. She spent 22 years living and working in Canada, qualifying her for CPP and OAS (having crossed the 20-year residency threshold) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:57">[00:07:57]</a>. She also received GIS due to modest income <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:06">[00:08:06]</a>. Portugal has a social security and tax treaty with Canada <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:10">[00:08:10]</a>.
>
> *   **CPP**: Continues without interruption <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:15">[00:08:15]</a>. Due to the tax treaty, she pays a reduced 15% withholding tax on her CPP <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:18">[00:08:18]</a>.
> *   **OAS**: Continues since she met the 20-year requirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:22">[00:08:22]</a>. Had she moved a year earlier, she would have received nothing until returning to Canada or qualifying through the treaty <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:24">[00:08:24]</a>.
> *   **GIS**: Stops after six months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:31">[00:08:31]</a>. GIS requires ongoing Canadian residency, with no workaround <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:35">[00:08:35]</a>.
>
> **What Anita did right**: She delayed her move until she hit 20 years of residency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:41">[00:08:41]</a>, chose a treaty country like Portugal to reduce tax <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:44">[00:08:44]</a>, and planned early to manage her budget without GIS <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:47">[00:08:47]</a>.

### Travel Companions Analogy
Think of CPP, OAS, and GIS as your travel companions when retiring abroad <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>:

*   **CPP**: Your "ride or die" friend. It sticks with you wherever you go, but watch out for [[tax_considerations_for_canadian_retirees_abroad | taxes]] at your destination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:04">[00:09:04]</a>.
*   **OAS**: More selective. It will only come if you've built a strong relationship (20+ years of living in Canada after age 18) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>. Otherwise, it might stay home unless you have "mutual friends" in countries with a social security agreement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:31">[00:09:31]</a>.
*   **GIS**: This friend loves Canada. They're generous while you're there, but once you leave for more than six months, they will stop <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:36">[00:09:36]</a>. There's no treaty, no workaround, and not even a goodbye <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:45">[00:09:45]</a>.
