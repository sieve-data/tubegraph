## The Craft of Product Management: Clarity and Conviction

### Clarity
Clarity in [[improving_product_management_skills | product management]] involves simplifying understanding, removing extraneous information, and bringing transparency to problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:30">[00:58:30]</a>. It's about defining the core problem that needs to be solved <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:02">[03:59:02]</a>.

Tactical approaches to achieving clarity include:
*   **"Understand Work"**: Actively using one's own product ("dogfooding") and competitor products ("catfooding") to identify issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:22:50">[03:22:50]</a>.
*   **"Top 10 Things You Should Know" Document**: A living document listing the 10 most critical problems associated with a product, which is revised quarterly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:51:00">[03:51:00]</a>. These problems can be qualitative, quantitative, or even technical debt <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:20:47">[03:20:47]</a>. This document ensures that the entire team, including engineering and design partners, has a shared understanding of the core issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:24:20">[03:24:20]</a>.
*   **Strategic Sessions**: Structured three-day workshops focused on insights, strategy, and "big rocks" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:02">[03:59:02]</a>.
    *   **Insights Day**: Dedicated to understanding the problems through exercises like app tear-downs and reviewing stakeholder input <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:27:00">[04:27:00]</a>.
    *   **Stakeholder Engagement**: Inviting stakeholders (marketing, operations, support, research, content strategy) to provide their "10 things you should know" to gain a multifaceted view of problems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:51:00">[03:51:00]</a>. This helps in sand-papering down to core issues <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:52:00">[03:52:00]</a>.
*   **Concise Writing**: Encouraging teams to write two-to-four page documents that outline insights, strategy (or approach), and the "big rocks" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:05">[00:59:05]</a>. This forces clarity in communication <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:16">[00:59:16]</a>.
*   **Prioritizing "Big Rocks"**: Focusing on three to five significant initiatives that, if delivered, would largely solve the identified problems, rather than an endless roadmap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:45">[00:59:45]</a>. These big rocks then inform the detailed roadmap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:03:10">[01:03:10]</a>.

### Conviction
Conviction is defined as a strong feeling or belief about what the world should be, not necessarily certainty or perfection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:15">[01:05:15]</a>. It’s about having a clear sense of what is right <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:31">[01:05:31]</a>.

To develop strong conviction:
*   **Pick a Lane**: Avoid spreading resources thinly across too many initiatives. Focus on the most impactful problem to solve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:06:51">[01:06:51]</a>.
*   **"Informed Captain"**: Identify a single person responsible for a decision, empowering them to gather context and input, but ultimately make the call <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:01">[01:21:01]</a>. This fosters accountability and requires the individual to stand by their choices <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:07:36">[01:07:36]</a>.

## Developing a Product Vision
A product vision is crucial at every level of a company, from junior PMs to directors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:26">[00:05:26]</a>. It serves as the "destination" for the product, akin to a pilot announcing the destination of a flight <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:01">[00:06:01]</a>.

### Vision vs. Mission
*   **Vision**: A picture of the future state once the problem is solved. It's a "fast-forward" to the destination <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:01">[00:12:01]</a>. Example: "A world where you get to this continuous trip so that you do not need parking" (Uber) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:22">[00:09:22]</a>.
*   **Mission**: The purpose or "why" behind the company's or product's existence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:24">[00:13:24]</a>. Example: "Make transportation reliable everywhere for everyone" (Uber) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:08">[00:09:08]</a>.

### Elements of a Good Vision
1.  **Lofty**: Inspiring and exciting, almost intimidating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:31">[00:06:31]</a>.
2.  **Realistic and Attainable**: Not so "pie-in-the-sky" that it feels out of reach for most people <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:46">[00:06:46]</a>.
3.  **Devoid of Current Limitations**: Imagined without constraints of today's technology or resources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:08">[00:07:08]</a>.
4.  **Grounded in a Clear Problem**: Directly addressing a potent user problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:08">[00:08:08]</a>.

### Communicating the Vision
Ab Ato offers three primary ways to communicate a product vision, leveraging the power of storytelling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:08">[00:15:08]</a>:

1.  **"Once Upon a Time" Mad Libs Framework**: A simple narrative structure:
    *   "Once Upon a Time [problem existed]" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:59">[00:15:59]</a>
    *   "And because of that [consequence]" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:56">[00:16:56]</a>
    *   "One day [solution/action happened]" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:02">[00:17:02]</a>
    *   "And as a result [new state of the world/impact]" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:06">[00:17:06]</a>
    *   This framework effectively tells a story of the problem, the intervention, and the resulting change <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:20:02">[00:20:02]</a>.

2.  **Future News Article/TechCrunch Article**: Write a hypothetical news article (or a TechCrunch headline) from the future where the vision has been realized <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:39">[00:17:39]</a>. This forces clarity on the impact and makes the vision tangible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:23:51">[00:23:51]</a>. This approach was used at Uber to envision a world where Uber could replace public transport cards <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:39">[00:22:39]</a>.

3.  **Visualization/Mock-ups**: Create visual representations of the future product. This can be as simple as hand-drawn sketches on Post-it notes, screenshots of a future App Store page, or even high-fidelity designs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:50">[00:24:50]</a>. The goal is to bring the narrative to life, even without a dedicated designer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:27:05">[00:27:05]</a>. An example is sketching a future Uber top-up kiosk for cash users <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:50">[00:18:50]</a>.

### Process for Developing and Evangelizing Vision
The development of a vision follows a three-step process:

1.  **Empathize**: Deeply understand the user and the problem <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>. This involves "understand work" (dogfooding, catfooding), leveraging user research to update mental models of the customer, and maintaining a "top 10 problems" document <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:15:00">[03:15:00]</a>. This empathy stage should be an ongoing, continuous effort, not just a one-off task <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:22:50">[03:22:50]</a>.
2.  **Create**: Formulate the vision using the frameworks above. This often happens in strategic sessions with key leaders (product, engineering, design, research, data science) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:14:00">[04:14:00]</a>. The vision should be a long-term goal, ideally spanning three to five years, and should not need yearly changes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:46">[00:49:46]</a>. Micro-visions, focused on shorter terms or specific problems, can also be created <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:00">[00:51:00]</a>.
3.  **Evangelize**: Share and gain buy-in for the vision across the organization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>.
    *   **Core Team**: Ensure the immediate product team understands and is committed to the vision. This involves repeated presentations and open discussion, allowing feedback and stress-testing of the vision <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:43:57">[00:43:57]</a>. Living documents with open comments are beneficial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:25">[00:45:25]</a>.
    *   **Stakeholders**: Share the vision with adjacent teams and managers who play a part in the product's success. This helps anticipate challenges and gain support <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:08">[00:46:08]</a>.
    *   **Leadership**: Present the vision to high-level leadership to gain their endorsement and amplify the message throughout the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:42">[00:46:42]</a>. The vision can even be used in hiring to excite candidates <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:48:42">[00:48:42]</a>.

## Product Culture and its Influence
The culture of a company significantly impacts how product is built <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:36">[01:08:36]</a>.

*   **Uber**: Characterized by a "monolithic" culture focused on hands-on operations, radical autonomy, "principal confrontation," and "toe-stepping" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:50">[01:09:50]</a>. This aggressive, "find your red line" mentality allowed rapid scaling and challenge of the status quo, even against initial resistance (e.g., enabling cash payments) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:42">[01:11:42]</a>.
*   **Netflix**: Known for its "no rules rules" framework, emphasizing "freedom of responsibility" and "highly aligned, loosely coupled" teams <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:31">[01:12:31]</a>. The culture encourages structured debates and intentional evolution of beliefs (e.g., pivoting to advertising-supported video on demand) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:14:56">[01:14:56]</a>.
*   **Google**: Possesses a looser macro-culture ("Googly" – respect the user, opportunity, each other) that allows for distinct "microcultures" within different product areas (e.g., Search, Cloud, Assistant) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:15:08">[01:15:08]</a>. This flexibility enables diverse product development approaches <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:16:48">[01:16:48]</a>.

### Building Team Culture
As a product leader, shaping team culture is vital. Key principles include:
*   **Freedom with Responsibility**: Granting autonomy but expecting accountability ("owner not a renter") <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:34">[01:20:34]</a>.
*   **Informed Captain**: Designating clear decision-makers to avoid consensus paralysis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:20:57">[01:20:57]</a>.
*   **Vulnerability as Strength**: Fostering an environment where team members feel safe to be human, with their flaws and aspirations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:43">[01:21:43]</a>. This builds trust and enables "love" rather than just being "liked," leading to more candid and constructive feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:22:00">[01:22:00]</a>.
*   **Human Connection**: Understanding team members' personal lives, goals, and even birthdays to build deeper relationships and foster collaboration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:35">[01:24:35]</a>.

## Advice for Aspiring Product Managers
*   **Diverse Backgrounds**: The best product managers often come from non-traditional backgrounds, as their different experiences provide valuable empathy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:29:50">[01:29:50]</a>.
*   **Proactive Learning**: Start practicing [[improving_product_management_skills | product management]] skills even before being hired. Identify problems in favorite apps, sketch solutions, and continually train your "product sense" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:06">[01:30:06]</a>. This builds expertise and ensures "opportunity meets preparation" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:30:29">[01:30:29]</a>.
