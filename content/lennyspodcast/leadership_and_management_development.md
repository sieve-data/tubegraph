Effective [[Leadership and management development]] hinges on strong communication and a culture of honest feedback. ## The Radical Candor Framework

Radical Candor occurs when you **care personally** and **challenge directly** at the same time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:06">[00:04:06]</a>. This framework can be visualized as a 2x2 matrix <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:19">[00:04:19]</a>:

*   **Obnoxious Aggression:** Occurs when you challenge directly but forget to show you care personally <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:31">[00:04:31]</a>. This approach hurts people and is inefficient because it can put others in a "fight or flight" mode, preventing them from hearing what you're saying <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:06">[00:05:06]</a>.
*   **Manipulative Insincerity:** The "worst place of all," it arises when, after realizing you've been obnoxiously aggressive, your instinct is to avoid challenging directly and pretend to agree when you disagree <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:37">[00:05:37]</a>.
*   **Ruinous Empathy:** This is the most common problem, occurring when you remember to show you care personally but are so worried about hurting someone's feelings that you fail to tell them something they need to know <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:11">[00:06:11]</a>. Kim Scott estimates that 90% of mistakes fall into this category <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:12">[00:07:12]</a>. Ruinous empathy creates a false harmony that is ultimately detrimental <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:10:01">[02:10:01]</a>.
*   **Radical Candor:** The ideal quadrant where both care and directness are present <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:06">[00:04:06]</a>.

The framework serves as a compass to guide specific conversations to a better place, helping individuals understand when they are moving in the wrong direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:09">[00:08:09]</a>.

## The Impact of Radical Candor

When individuals get better at Radical Candor, they build better relationships, do better work, become more successful, and are happier <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:49">[00:08:49]</a>.

### Case Study: The "Um" Story

Upon joining Google in 2004, Kim Scott presented to the founders and CEO <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:15">[00:09:15]</a>. After a successful presentation, her boss, rather than offering a high-five, invited Kim to her office <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:15">[00:10:15]</a>. The boss praised Kim's presentation but then directly told her she said "um" a lot, making her sound "stupid" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:53">[00:10:53]</a>. While the feedback was harsh, Kim recognized it as the kindest thing her boss could have done at that moment in her career <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:34">[00:11:34]</a>. The boss knew Kim well enough (cared personally) to use those specific words to get through to her (challenged directly), leading Kim to seek a speech coach and improve <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:42">[00:11:42]</a>.

This [[lessons_in_leadership_and_team_management | leadership lesson]] highlighted two key aspects of her boss's [[effective_leadership_and_strategic_thinking | effective leadership]]:
*   **Caring Personally:** The boss cared about Kim as a human being, demonstrated by supporting her when her father was diagnosed with cancer <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:45">[00:12:45]</a>. This kind of care, even if it doesn't scale to an entire organization, creates a [[leadership_and_company_culture | culture of caring]] among direct reports <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:30">[00:13:30]</a>.
*   **Challenging Directly:** Kim quickly learned that if she "screwed up," her boss would tell her and keep telling her until the message "penetrated" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:51">[00:13:51]</a>.

### The "Bob" Story: Consequences of Ruinous Empathy

Kim once hired a charismatic but underperforming employee named Bob <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:11:09">[02:11:09]</a>. Despite his poor work, Kim, worried about hurting his feelings and her own reputation, avoided direct feedback, saying things like, "Oh Bob, this is such a great start, you're so awesome, we all love working with you. Maybe you can make it just a little bit better" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:23">[02:22:23]</a>. This "ruinous empathy" continued for 10 months <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:48:06">[02:48:06]</a>. Eventually, Kim realized she had to fire Bob, not only because it was unfair to him not to receive honest feedback, but also because it was unfair to the rest of the team <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:48:58">[02:48:58]</a>. Their deliverables were delayed, and high-performing team members were frustrated and considering quitting because they couldn't do their best work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:46:01">[02:46:01]</a>.

When Kim finally spoke to Bob about his performance, he asked, "Why didn't you tell me? I thought you all cared about me" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:50:41">[02:50:41]</a>. Kim realized that by trying to be "nice," she had ultimately caused him to be fired, which was "not so nice after all" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:56:06">[02:56:06]</a>. This painful experience underscored the importance of honest feedback for both individual growth and team performance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:59:16">[02:59:16]</a>.

## Tactical Advice for Giving Feedback (HIP Core)

When giving feedback, whether praise or criticism, consider these elements <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:43:08">[01:43:08]</a>:

*   **Humble:** Approach conversations with humility, recognizing you might be wrong. Candor is about dialogue, not stating "the truth" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:54:19">[01:54:19]</a>.
*   **Helpful:** State your intention to be helpful; feedback is meant to assist the other person, not dominate them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:08:44">[02:08:44]</a>.
*   **Immediately:** Give feedback as soon as possible. Delaying often means never giving it at all <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:14:48">[02:14:48]</a>. The purpose of praise is to encourage more of a behavior, and criticism is to reduce a behavior, so waiting doesn't make sense <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:01">[02:17:01]</a>. Exceptions are if you or the other person are too upset to communicate effectively <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:07">[02:17:07]</a>.
*   **In-person/Synchronously:** Have these conversations synchronously, ideally in person or via phone, to gauge how the feedback is landing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:17:14">[02:17:14]</a>. Email, text, or Slack are not recommended for feedback as they lack the nuance of real-time interaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:18:19">[02:18:19]</a>.
*   **Praise in Public, Criticize in Private:** This general rule helps reinforce positive behavior publicly while addressing issues more privately <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:06:50">[02:06:50]</a>.
*   **Not about Personality:** Focus feedback on observable behaviors and their impact, not on personal attributes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:00">[02:09:00]</a>.

Kim recommends using the **CORE** model for structuring feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:15">[01:59:15]</a>:
*   **C**ontext: Where and when did it happen? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:15">[01:59:15]</a>
*   **O**bservation: What did the person do or say? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:15">[01:59:15]</a>
*   **R**esult: What was the impact of their action or words? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:59:15">[01:59:15]</a>
*   **E**xtra Step (Next Step): What should they do differently (or more of) next time? <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:02">[02:00:02]</a>

## Tactical Advice for Soliciting Feedback

Soliciting criticism is the first and most important step to improving relationships and making it easier to give and receive feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:20:00">[03:20:00]</a>.

1.  **Ask a Good Question:** Avoid generic questions like "Do you have any feedback for me?" as they often yield "Everything's fine" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:51:00">[03:51:00]</a>. Instead, ask something that elicits a response. Kim's preferred question is: "What could I do or stop doing that would make it easier to work with me?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:51:00">[03:51:00]</a>. The question must sound authentic to you <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:51:00">[03:51:00]</a>. Other examples include: "Tell me why I'm wrong" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:46:00">[03:46:00]</a>, "What's one thing I could be doing better?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:09:00">[04:09:00]</a>, or "What could I have done this week to better support you in your work?" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:24:00">[04:24:00]</a>.
2.  **Embrace Discomfort and Wait (Count to Six):** There's no emotional Novocain for feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:34:00">[04:34:00]</a>. After asking, close your mouth and count to six. Most people cannot endure six seconds of silence and will offer something <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:39:00">[04:39:00]</a>.
3.  **Listen with Intent to Understand:** Your natural reaction might be defensiveness, and that's normal <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:41:00">[04:41:00]</a>. Ask follow-up questions to truly understand what the person is saying, rather than immediately formulating a response <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:51:00">[04:51:00]</a>.
4.  **Reward the Candor:** People take a huge risk when giving critical feedback, especially to a boss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:44:00">[04:44:00]</a>. If you agree with the feedback, fix the problem and make your listening tangible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:58:00">[04:58:00]</a>. Be loud about fixing it to show that their feedback is valued <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:03:00">[05:03:00]</a>.
    *   **If you disagree:** Acknowledge the part you can agree with (even 5-10%), and then say you'll think about the rest and get back to them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:56:00">[04:56:00]</a>. Crucially, *get back to them* with a respectful explanation of your disagreement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:56:00">[04:56:00]</a>. Unspoken disagreement is more damaging to relationships than respectful disagreement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:56:00">[04:56:00]</a>.

### Frequency of Soliciting Feedback

*   **Managers:** Solicit feedback weekly from each direct report, budgeting five minutes at the end of every one-on-one <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:29:00">[03:29:00]</a>.
*   **All Employees:** Ask your go-to question of cross-functional peers you work closely with, and of your boss <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:13:00">[04:13:00]</a>. This should be part of a daily/weekly routine <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:27:00">[04:27:00]</a>.

## Addressing Specific Challenges in Leadership and Management Development

### Overcoming the Need to Be Liked

Many leaders, especially "people-pleasers," struggle with giving hard feedback because they want to be liked <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:57:00">[02:57:00]</a>. Kim suggests:
*   **Focus on Caring, Not Liking:** Realize your job is to care about others and to become "others-focused," which helps let go of the need to be liked <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:22:00">[02:22:00]</a>.
*   **Long-Term Kindness:** Understand that it's "unkind" not to tell someone something they need to know in the long run <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:09:00">[02:09:00]</a>. This reframes directness as a form of kindness.
*   **Focus on Their Goals:** If you know someone wants to get promoted, but a specific behavior is holding them back, framing feedback around their [[career_development_for_product_owners_and_managers | career development]] and goals can be highly effective <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:39:00">[03:39:00]</a>. Having meaningful [[career_development_for_product_owners_and_managers | career conversations]] demonstrates care <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:57:00">[03:57:00]</a>.

### Dealing with Resistant Company Cultures

If your [[leadership_and_company_culture | company culture]] is closer to ruinous empathy and people aren't ready for direct feedback, the solution is to begin with soliciting criticism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:16:00">[03:16:00]</a>. This is the first and most important step to improving relationships <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:20:00">[03:20:00]</a>.

### Dealing with Low Self-Awareness Leaders

For leaders who exhibit obnoxious aggression or ignore problems, the most important thing is to explain the impact they are having <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:00">[03:59:00]</a>. Frame the feedback in terms of "enlightened self-interest," highlighting how their behavior affects their own results and career <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:00">[03:59:00]</a>. Often, these leaders do care about results, even if they're bad at showing personal care <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:59:00">[03:59:00]</a>. Sharing personal stories of your own mistakes can help them gain self-awareness and adopt a growth mindset <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:09:00">[04:09:00]</a>.

### Authentic Leadership Style

Developing an authentic leadership style means understanding that while being polite is important, being kind, in the long run, sometimes means giving direct feedback <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:11">[01:08:11]</a>. It's about focusing on doing the right thing for people and truly caring about them more than your own discomfort in the moment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:20">[01:09:20]</a>.

*   **"It's not mean, it's clear."** This motto encapsulates the essence of Radical Candor â€“ clarity born from care <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:21:43">[01:21:43]</a>.
