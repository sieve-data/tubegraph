The concept of "beauty" in business and product development has seen a dramatic decrease in usage in digitized books over time, aligning with the idea that "functionality is King" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:00">[00:00:00]</a>. However, this perspective often wrongly positions functionality and beauty as opposing forces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:16">[00:00:16]</a>. In reality, beauty enhances functionality by making products easier to use, more approachable, and more compelling <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:22">[00:00:22]</a>.

## The Business Value of Beauty and Quality

Katie Dill, Head of Design at Stripe, emphasizes that beauty is not separate from functionality; it actively contributes to business success <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:22:51">[00:22:51]</a>.

Key benefits of integrating beauty and quality into product design include:
*   **Increased Trust** Beautifully designed products communicate painstaking detail and care, assuring users that the company cares about other, less visible details, thereby increasing trust <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:38">[00:00:38]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:23">[00:24:23]</a>.
*   **Enhanced Usability and Desirability** Beauty makes things easier to use, more accessible, and leads to better user outcomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:37">[00:24:37]</a>. This means a more enjoyable and user-centric experience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:21:25">[00:21:25]</a>.
*   **"Beauty Begets Beauty"** High-quality design inspires similar attention to detail from users and partners, reflecting positively on the company's care and creativity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:01">[00:25:01]</a>.
*   **Pride and Talent Attraction** Pursuing quality and beauty fosters a sense of pride within the team, making the company a more attractive place for talented individuals who care about their craft <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:47">[00:25:47]</a>.

### Quality as Growth

A critical insight is that quality is not separate from growth; it directly drives it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:47">[00:15:47]</a>.
When products are easier to use and more understandable, it naturally encourages more people to use them and use them more frequently <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:52">[00:15:52]</a>.

Stripe's growth team, for example, is intensely focused on building better experiences because it directly ties to business metrics <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:11">[00:16:11]</a>.
*   **Onboarding Flow Improvements**: Enhancing the onboarding experience to make products easier to understand has led to increased activations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:22">[00:16:22]</a>.
*   **Checkout Experience**: Research on top e-commerce sites revealed that 99% had errors in their checkout flow hindering seamless transactions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:50">[00:16:50]</a>. By maniacally focusing on improving the checkout experience through design details, Stripe saw a **10.5% increase in businesses' revenue** when moving from older to newer checkout forms <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:22">[00:17:22]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:38">[00:17:38]</a>. This demonstrates a direct [[quality_improvement_leading_to_business_impact | business impact]] from quality improvements.

> "I think it's actually a false belief that you know it's like one or the other it's like are we going to work on quality and it doesn't move the metrics." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:49:55">[00:49:55]</a>

### Levels of Quality
Quality exists on a spectrum <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:55">[00:11:55]</a>:
1.  **Baseline**: The product works and provides value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:59">[00:11:59]</a>.
2.  **Error-Free**: The product performs exceedingly well and is well-rounded <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:09">[00:12:09]</a>.
3.  **Exceeding Expectations**: The product does something the user wasn't even seeking, often surpassing user expectations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:20">[00:12:20]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:26">[00:24:26]</a>.

Companies that consider quality non-negotiable understand that it is fundamental to features and leads to great usability and desirability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:30">[00:13:30]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:42">[00:13:42]</a>. This is analogous to working out: consistent effort adds up to better long-term outcomes, even if a single session doesn't show immediate results <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:54">[00:13:54]</a>.

## Operationalizing Quality and Design

Achieving great design requires a concerted, ongoing effort, as there's a natural "gravitational pull to mediocrity" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:53">[00:26:53]</a>.

Stripe's approach to operationalizing [[design_and_innovation_in_software_development | design and innovation]] and quality includes:
1.  **Group Effort**: Quality is a collective responsibility, not just one person's or one team's job <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:31">[00:29:31]</a>. Internal standards and communication are crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:00">[00:30:00]</a>.
2.  **Vision and Alignment**: Even with talented people, a shared vision is necessary to ensure individual contributions coalesce into a coherent, high-quality whole, similar to how an architect ensures all parts of a house fit together <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:30:22">[00:30:22]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:05">[00:31:05]</a>.
3.  **Editing**: Having an "editor" or a lead with the ability to narrow, reduce, and remove elements that don't fit the overall vision is vital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:21">[00:31:21]</a>.
4.  **Courage**: Leaders must possess the courage to say "no, this isn't good enough" even when teams have put significant effort into something <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:55">[00:31:55]</a>.
5.  **User-Centric Understanding**: Quality must be understood from the user's perspective, considering their entire journey, not just isolated product features <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:37">[00:32:37]</a>.

### "Walk the Store" Process
To combat quality regression over time, Stripe implemented a "Walk the Store" program <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:46">[00:33:46]</a>.
*   **Focus on Journeys**: 15 critical user journeys are identified <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:56">[00:34:56]</a>.
*   **Cross-Functional Ownership**: Each journey has dedicated engineering, product, and [[design_and_innovation_in_software_development | design]] leaders responsible for its quality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:22">[00:35:22]</a>.
*   **Regular Review**: These leaders regularly "friction log" their experiences as if they were users, noting what works and what doesn't, from initial search to dashboard interaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:32">[00:35:32]</a>.
*   **Scoring and Calibration**: Teams score the quality of their journeys using a rubric focused on usability, utility, and desirability <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:45:15">[00:45:15]</a>. These scores are then calibrated in multidisciplinary "product quality review" (PQR) meetings to ensure a shared understanding of the quality bar across the company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:33">[00:36:33]</a>.
*   **Impact**: This process increases awareness and accountability, highlights upstream impacts (e.g., how SEO affects later stages of a journey), and fosters a company-wide commitment to quality <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:40">[00:37:40]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:47">[00:38:47]</a>. It validates personal judgment in addition to data and user research <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:39:20">[00:39:20]</a>.

### Fighting the Fear of Bold Ideas
Companies often exhibit a "fear of bold ideas," preferring incremental changes that are easier to measure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:50">[00:57:50]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:01">[01:08:01]</a>. However, focusing only on small, measurable improvements within limited scopes can prevent making the entire user experience better <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:08:50">[01:08:50]</a>.

Dill advocates for "reach for the stars and land on the Moon" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:02">[01:09:02]</a>.
*   **Vision Work**: This involves sketching out the ideal, comprehensive "11-star experience" (as coined by Brian Chesky of Airbnb) for the entire user journey <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:06">[01:09:06]</a>, <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:37">[01:09:37]</a>.
*   **Strategic Incrementation**: While the vision is grand, the implementation can be iterative, shipping smaller pieces to learn and gather data <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:29">[01:10:29]</a>. This North Star approach ensures that incremental steps lead towards a cohesive, impactful long-term outcome <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:10:40">[01:10:40]</a>.

## Building and Scaling Design Teams

### Performance Formula
Katie Dill uses the formula: **Performance = Potential - Interference** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:05">[00:55:05]</a>.
*   **Increase Potential**: Achieved through strong hiring, talent development, and growth opportunities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:37">[00:55:37]</a>.
*   **Decrease Interference**: Removing obstacles that hinder great work, such as outdated processes, misalignment, or poor collaboration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:50">[00:55:50]</a>.

### Combating Information Silos
As companies grow, people can lose touch with what's happening across the organization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:14">[00:57:14]</a>.
*   **Visual Sharing**: Design teams at Stripe, and previously at Airbnb, regularly share screenshots or prototypes of their work in a shared deck (like Google Slides) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:57:47">[00:57:47]</a>. This allows designers to see what others are building and identify opportunities for collaboration or consistent patterns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:07">[00:58:07]</a>. It also provides product managers, engineering leaders, and other company leaders with a clear visual overview of ongoing development <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:20">[00:58:20]</a>. This helps [[understanding_the_challenges_of_developing_effective_business_strategies | align strategy]] and prevents redundant work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:01:16">[01:01:16]</a>.

### Reorganizing for Collaboration
At Lyft, Dill faced a situation where the design team was physically separated from engineering and product, leading to wasted work and misalignment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:02:19">[01:02:19]</a>. While this dedicated "creative space" had benefits, it created interference.
The solution was to physically and organizationally integrate design with their cross-functional partners <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:16">[01:04:16]</a>. Designers were embedded within product teams (e.g., working on driver, rider, or safety teams) and sat alongside their respective engineers and product managers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:04:47">[01:04:47]</a>. This fostered faster iteration cycles and clearer understanding of shared goals, while still maintaining dedicated creative spaces for specific design activities <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:05:01">[01:05:01]</a>. This reflects [[creative_approaches_to_building_beloved_b2b_products | creative approaches to building products]] and [[challenges_with_rebranding_and_redesigning_for_growth | rebranding and redesigning for growth]].

### Hiring for Design
When hiring designers, prioritize:
*   **Taste and Character**: These are harder to teach than tools or processes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:24">[01:11:24]</a>. Look for a natural inclination towards great things <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:11:52">[01:11:52]</a>.
*   **Humility**: A humble designer is more attuned to user needs and curious about improving the product <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:09">[01:12:09]</a>.
*   **Hustle/Courage**: The act of creation is inherently scary. Look for individuals who have the courage to propose new ideas and fight for excellence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:12:47">[01:12:47]</a>.

For startups, while a "doer" is often needed initially, it's also important to have a strategic mindset. This might mean hiring a senior design advisor alongside a full-time executor to ensure a user-focused approach from the start <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:27">[01:13:27]</a>, relating to [[creative_branding_strategies_for_startups | creative branding strategies for startups]].
