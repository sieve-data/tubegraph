## Defining [[Behavioral Public Policy | Behavioral Public Policy]]

[[Behavioral Public Policy | Behavioral Public Policy]] involves using [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | behavioral science]] evidence to inform policymaking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:44">[00:07:44]</a>. It helps governments and policymakers:
*   **Problem Identification**: Notice and prioritize societal challenges where behaviors are counter to long-term interests (e.g., unhealthy food choices, non-utilization of government programs) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:59">[00:07:59]</a>. It also aids in analyzing problems and understanding behavioral drivers and barriers within social systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:15">[00:08:15]</a>.
*   **Policy Design**: Use [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | behavioral science]] evidence to design policies and programs that address identified barriers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:22">[00:08:22]</a>.
*   **Policy Assessment**: Utilize tools like randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and pilot testing to de-risk policy implementation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:39">[00:08:39]</a>.
*   **Policy Implementation**: Optimize existing programs, maximize impact, and ensure intended outcomes are realized in practice <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:51">[00:08:51]</a>.
*   **Monitoring and Evaluation**: Help policymakers monitor and evaluate policy implementation to inform future problem identification <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:10">[00:09:10]</a>.

## Country Experiences and Lessons Learned

### Finland
Finland's journey in [[Behavioral Public Policy | behavioral science]] started around 2013 with government collaborations and experimentation culture <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:24:02">[00:24:02]</a>. A key turning point was a pilot project in October 2020 during the COVID-19 crisis, which advised communications teams on public behavior related to masks and vaccinations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:25:17">[00:25:17]</a>. Successful trials, like using text messages to encourage voting, led to team growth and expansion into various subject areas across ministries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:26:44">[00:26:44]</a>. The Finnish team emphasizes the importance of a good team, strong support from the Prime Minister's Office, and the belief that not using [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | behavioral science]] is no longer an option <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:28:32">[00:28:32]</a>. They strive to be ambitious yet humble, working collaboratively with civil servants rather than acting as consultants <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:29:08">[00:29:08]</a>.

A major lesson learned is that building [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | behavioral science]] into government work takes significant time and resources, with no shortcuts <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:29">[00:50:29]</a>. It requires working closely within the specific national context and understanding internal processes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:50:50">[00:50:50]</a>. Effective communication and regular engagement (e.g., coffee mornings, research presentations) are vital to build trust and show people the ongoing work <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:51:33">[00:51:33]</a>.

### United Kingdom (HMRC)
The UK's first [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | behavioral insights team]] (Nudge Unit) began its trials in HMRC (the tax authority), demonstrating quantifiable impact with trials like "nine out of ten people in your area paid their tax on time" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:31:50">[00:31:50]</a>. Initial success benefited from huge political support, which provided clout but was time-limited <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:32:06">[00:32:06]</a>. A turning point was when political support shifted, leading the team to find success in "side streams"â€”areas with more autonomy and less status quo to fight against, such as debt management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:33:37">[00:33:37]</a>. This allowed for building strong relationships, training junior staff, and conducting a series of trials on complex behaviors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:34:21">[00:34:21]</a>.

Another significant turning point was the COVID-19 pandemic, where the [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | behavioral science team]] had a widespread impact on the Furlough scheme <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:35:11">[00:35:11]</a>. Factors enabling this thorough [[Incorporating Behavioral Science in Government and Industry | behavioral input]] included:
*   **Novelty**: Being a new scheme meant no established norms, creating openness for new ideas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:36:48">[00:36:48]</a>.
*   **Track Record**: The team's established credibility and relationships meant they were trusted to sit at the "top table" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:12">[00:37:12]</a>.
*   **Senior Buy-in**: The Chancellor at the time (Rishi Sunak) was behaviorally astute and open to behavioral perspectives <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:37:50">[00:37:50]</a>.
*   **Opportunism**: The team was proactive in seeking out opportunities, knowing the organization well, and having strong communicators to demonstrate value <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:38:15">[00:38:15]</a>.

A key lesson learned is the challenge of maintaining subtlety in [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | behavioral science]] interventions when scaling them up within large government organizations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:53:56">[00:53:56]</a>. Often, the delicate design of interventions can be lost in the "machine" of training hundreds of staff and adapting to broad government systems <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:54:31">[00:54:31]</a>.

### Brazil
Brazil's [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | behavioral science]] journey began with isolated experiences, gaining momentum through the National School of Public Administration since 2017 with regular courses and publications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:40">[00:40:40]</a>. A significant turning point was the inception of Cinco in 2023, the first [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | behavioral science]] unit established within Brazil's new Ministry of Management and Innovation in Public Services <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:41:49">[00:41:49]</a>. This provides a unique opportunity for [[Behavioral Public Policy | behavioral science]] to influence transversal policies across the entire public administration <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:42:25">[00:42:25]</a>.

The success of Cinco was driven by advocacy, the supportive new government structure, and a strong innovation movement within Brazil <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:46:01">[00:46:01]</a>. The team, comprising 15 full-time civil servants from diverse backgrounds, emphasizes working *with* policymakers to develop solutions rather than simply advising them <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:44:02">[00:44:02]</a>. This collaborative approach fosters a sense of belonging and helps address complex problems where traditional methods have failed, such as mosquito-borne diseases <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:47:09">[00:47:09]</a>. A key lesson is the importance of learning from more experienced practitioners and dealing with non-technical, political issues unique to their country's context <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:55:46">[00:55:46]</a>.

## OECD's Future Role in Reviewing [[Behavioral Public Policy | Behavioral Public Policy]] Capacity

The OECD plans to use the Logic principles to conduct country reviews on how nations organize and manage their use of [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | behavioral science]] to improve policies and services <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:58:03">[00:58:03]</a>. This aims to help governments worldwide improve their application of [[Behavioral Insights for Public Policy | behavioral science]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:59:50">[00:59:50]</a>.
