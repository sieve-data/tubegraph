## Historical Discoveries of Synchrony

### Huygens' Pendulum Clocks

In 1656, Dutch physicist Christian Huygens created the first working pendulum clock, aiming to help sailors determine their longitude at sea <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:08">[00:02:08]</a>. His pendulum clocks were accurate to within 10 to 15 seconds per day, a significant improvement over earlier clocks that could be off by 15 minutes a day <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:38">[00:02:38]</a>. Huygens' plan involved attaching two clocks to a heavy hanging mass on a ship to prevent them from being affected by rough seas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:44">[00:02:44]</a>.

During testing in February 1665, while sick at home, Huygens made a remarkable discovery <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:57">[00:02:57]</a>. He observed that two of his clocks, hung from the same wooden beam across chairs, would spontaneously synchronize after about half an hour <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:03">[00:03:03]</a>. When one pendulum swung one way, the other would swing the opposite way, and their ticks would align <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:16">[00:03:16]</a>. Even after disturbing them to tick out of sync, they would return to the same lockstep within 30 minutes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:26">[00:03:26]</a>.

Huygens initially hypothesized that air currents were causing this "strange sympathy," but placing a board between the pendulums did not stop the synchronization <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:37">[00:03:37]</a>. He realized that the clocks synchronized because they were hung from the same wooden beam, which transferred mechanical vibrations between them, effectively coupling the two oscillators <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:00">[00:04:00]</a>. Huygens was the first to observe this spontaneous [[Mechanical and Human Synchrony in Structures | synchronization]] in inanimate objects <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:11">[00:04:11]</a>.

## Mechanical Synchrony in Human Structures

### The Millennium Bridge Wobble

The Millennium Bridge in London, opened to the public, began to wobble significantly as crowds filled it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:39">[00:00:39]</a>. This led to its closure just two days later, not reopening for another two years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:58">[00:00:58]</a>. This incident highlighted a new understanding of how human movement can interact with structures.

Engineers have long known that armies should break step when crossing bridges, a practice dating back to an accident in 1831 when 74 men from the 60th Rifle Corps caused the Broughton Suspension Bridge in northern England to collapse under their synchronized footsteps <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:08">[00:01:08]</a>. The British Army subsequently ordered troops to break step on bridges <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:34">[00:01:34]</a>.

The Millennium Bridge incident was different because the public was not consciously marching in step <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:40">[00:01:40]</a>. The bridge was designed to have a unique construction, with supporting cables running alongside it like taut guitar strings <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:27">[00:15:27]</a>. Civil engineers know that a footbridge should not have a resonant frequency equal to the vertical frequency of human walking (about two strides per second or 2 hertz) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:40">[00:15:40]</a>. The Millennium Bridge designers accounted for this <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:00">[00:16:00]</a>.

However, they did not know about the importance of a resonant frequency at half that, one cycle per second (1 hertz), which is the frequency with which a person puts down one foot (e.g., the left foot) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:06">[00:16:06]</a>. When walking, people exert a small sideways force on a bridge, typically about one-tenth of their downward force <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:22">[00:16:22]</a>. Normally, these sideways forces are negligible because people walk at different paces <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:31">[00:16:31]</a>. But if the bridge has a lateral resonant frequency of one cycle per second, as the Millennium Bridge did, these small forces can cause the bridge to start moving <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:41">[00:16:41]</a>.

Tests showed that when around 166 people were on the bridge, a dramatic increase in acceleration occurred, causing it to sway like on opening day, representing a phase transition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:16">[00:17:16]</a>. If a platform starts moving sideways, people instinctively spread their legs apart to stabilize themselves and tend to walk in step with the sideways motion of the bridge <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:32">[00:17:32]</a>. Thus, it was the wobbling bridge that *caused* people to walk in sync, not the other way around <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:17:58">[00:17:58]</a>. This created a positive feedback loop: the crowd's motion caused the bridge to move more, leading more people to synchronize with the bridge, further increasing its motion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:03">[00:18:03]</a>. The problem was solved by installing energy-dissipating dampers along the bridge, which decreased the coupling strength <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:18:31">[00:18:31]</a>.

## Explaining Synchronization: The [[Kuramoto Model and Synchronization Theory | Kuramoto Model]]

The spontaneous [[Mechanical and Human Synchrony in Structures | synchronization]] seen in phenomena like metronomes placed on a wobbly platform can be understood by considering how the platform's motion influences individual oscillators <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:30">[00:04:30]</a>. When multiple metronomes are in sync, their large masses accelerate in unison, pushing the platform in one direction, then the other, keeping the system's center of mass roughly stable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:16">[00:05:16]</a>. If an out-of-sync metronome is introduced, the platform's motion provides a kick at each half-swing, speeding it up until it aligns with the others <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:31">[00:05:31]</a>. This principle scales up, with the platform moving in the direction of the majority of metronomes <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:59">[00:05:59]</a>.

The position of an oscillator, such as a metronome pendulum, can be represented as a point on a circle, indicating its phase in the cycle <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:08">[00:06:08]</a>. When oscillators synchronize in phase, their points move around the circle together <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:46">[00:06:46]</a>.

This behavior is described by the [[Kuramoto Model and Synchronization Theory | Kuramoto Model]], a mathematical model where the rate at which each dot (oscillator) moves around the circle is determined by its natural frequency plus an amount related to its phase difference from all other dots <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:57">[00:06:57]</a>. The "coupling strength" determines the influence of this phase difference <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:10">[00:07:10]</a>. An analogy is two friends running on a track: if the coupling (their friendship/effort) is strong enough, the faster friend might slow down or the slower friend might speed up, overcoming their inherent speed differences to run together <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:15">[00:07:15]</a>. If the coupling is weak, one person will eventually lap the other <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:44">[00:07:44]</a>.

Synchronization often occurs as a phase transition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:01">[00:09:01]</a>. Similar to how water suddenly freezes at a critical temperature rather than gradually, oscillators "lock" their phases in time once a critical level of coupling is surpassed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:06">[00:09:06]</a>. This "crystallization in time" is the phenomenon of [[Mechanical and Human Synchrony in Structures | synchronization]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:28">[00:09:28]</a>.

## Manifestations of Spontaneous Order

[[Synchronization in Chemical and Biological Systems | Synchronization]] is a universal phenomenon observed across every scale of nature, from subatomic to cosmic, and utilizes every communication channel, including gravitational, electrical, chemical, and mechanical interactions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:02">[00:10:02]</a>.

### Biological Synchrony

*   **Fireflies**: Fireflies in Southeast Asia synchronize their flashes, even though each firefly has its own natural flashing frequency <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:53">[00:07:53]</a>. Their strong coupling allows hundreds or thousands to flash simultaneously <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:04">[00:08:04]</a>. In simulations, even with small, short-range interactions where a firefly nudges its internal clock forward if it sees a nearby flash, waves of synchronization travel through the population, eventually leading to all fireflies flashing at once <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:31">[00:08:31]</a>.
*   **Human Applause**: Audiences can spontaneously synchronize their applause after a performance, demonstrating a visible phase transition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:36">[00:09:36]</a>.
*   **Heartbeat**: The regular beating of the human heart is another example of spontaneous order <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:24">[00:00:24]</a>.

### Cosmic Synchrony

*   **Tidal Locking**: Earth's Moon always shows one side because it rotates on its axis exactly once for every orbit around Earth, a phenomenon called tidal locking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:32">[00:10:32]</a>. This is common in the solar system, with 34 moons tidally locked to their planets <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:43">[00:10:43]</a>. Tidal locking occurs because the gravitational attraction from the planet distorts the moon into an egg shape <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:55">[00:10:55]</a>. As the moon orbits and rotates, these bulges move out of alignment with the planet, causing gravitational forces to constantly pull them back, slowing (or speeding up) the moon's rotation until it locks into sync <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:05">[00:11:05]</a>.
*   **Orbital Resonance**: Beyond tidal locking, other beautiful [[synchronization_in_chemical_and_biological_systems | synchronization]] phenomena exist, such as the orbital resonance of Jupiter's three innermost moonsâ€”Io, Europa, and Ganymede <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:33">[00:11:33]</a>. For every orbit of Ganymede, Europa completes two, and Io completes four <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:40">[00:11:40]</a>.

### Chemical Synchrony

*   **Belousov-Zhabotinsky (BZ) Reaction**: In the 1950s, Russian chemists sought a chemical reaction that would oscillate like a chemical pendulum, changing colors repeatedly <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:56">[00:11:56]</a>. While the [[Second Law of Thermodynamics and Disorder | second law of thermodynamics]] dictates that closed systems increase their [[The Arrow of Time and Entropy | entropy]] and move towards equilibrium, there is no principle preventing them from oscillating and damping out to equilibrium in an oscillatory manner <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:13">[00:12:13]</a>. Boris Belousov and Anatol Zhabotinsky discovered such a reaction, now known as the BZ reaction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:35">[00:12:35]</a>. This reaction can oscillate between colors for half an hour or more, showcasing quadrillions of molecules acting collectively <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:48">[00:12:48]</a>.
*   **Chemical Waves**: If the BZ reaction is unstirred in a petri dish, it produces mesmerizing spiral waves or expanding target patterns of color <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:21">[00:13:21]</a>. These "chemical waves" are changes in chemical concentrations, not physical movement of the liquid, propagating at a constant speed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:39">[00:13:39]</a>.
*   **Connection to Cardiac Arrhythmias**: The spiral waves observed in the BZ reaction closely resemble spiral waves of electrical excitation found in the heart <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:02">[00:14:02]</a>. Art Winfree, inspired by these chemical reaction waves, applied this insight to understand cardiac arrhythmias, particularly ventricular fibrillation, which is caused by a lack of [[synchronization_in_chemical_and_biological_systems | synchronization]] in the heart <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:18">[00:14:18]</a>. His work contributed to theories on the causes of fibrillation and the design of better defibrillators <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:40">[00:14:40]</a>.
