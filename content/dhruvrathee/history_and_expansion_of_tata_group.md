## Early Beginnings: Nusserwanji Tata

The story of Tata Group begins in 1822 with the birth of Nusserwanji Tata in a Gujarati village to a family of Parsi priests <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:28">[00:01:28]</a>. Driven by ambition, Nusserwanji moved to Mumbai at age 20 to establish a new business, a significant step considering he had a wife and child at the time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:57">[00:01:57]</a>. He ventured into the cotton export business, ensuring his son Jamsetji received the best English education available <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:18">[00:02:18]</a>. Nusserwanji demonstrated dedication to his business and investors, once selling his mansion to repay them when the cotton business faced difficulties <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:25">[00:07:25]</a>.

## Jamsetji Tata's Vision and Expansion

Jamsetji, equipped with his education, was sent to Hong Kong in 1859 by his father to expand the business, an arduous journey by ship before air travel was invented <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:47">[00:02:47]</a>. Jamsetji's ambition led him to immense success, working across three continents and establishing numerous cotton mills <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:38">[00:03:38]</a>. He also laid the groundwork for India's first steel factory and launched India's first 5-star hotel, the Mumbai [[history_of_the_taj_mahal | Taj Hotel]], notable for being the first fully electrified hotel in India <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:43">[00:03:43]</a>.

Jamsetji Tata, who passed away in 1904 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:06">[00:04:06]</a>, is remembered not just for building profitable businesses, but for pioneering [[jamsetji_tatas_business_ethics_and_innovations | principles and ethics]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:24">[00:04:24]</a>.

### Ethical Business Practices

In 1874, when Jamsetji established his first cotton mill in Nagpur (named the Empress Mill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:02">[00:06:02]</a>), he observed worker laziness and absenteeism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:44">[00:04:44]</a>. Instead of reprimanding them, he sought a humane solution:
*   He established a General Provident Fund to ensure pensions for laborers after retirement <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:21">[00:05:21]</a>.
*   He initiated an insurance scheme to cover medical costs for accident-stricken workers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:30">[00:05:30]</a>.
*   He organized Family Days and Sports Days to foster community among workers and their families <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:39">[00:05:39]</a>.

These practices, common today, were revolutionary in the 1800s, when working conditions globally were often terrible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:48">[00:05:48]</a>. Jamsetji personally bore these expenses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:05">[00:06:05]</a>.

### Challenges and Persistence

During the American Civil War (1861-1865), which disrupted cotton supply to England, Jamsetji seized the opportunity to double his cotton rates, leading to huge profits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:13">[00:06:13]</a>. However, when the war ended and American supplies resumed, his business faced losses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:41">[00:06:41]</a>. Despite investor pressure, Jamsetji's steadfast honesty and promise to repay them, even while accepting a meager £20 monthly salary as owner, allowed him to protect the business and his reputation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:54">[00:06:54]</a>.

### Dream Projects

Before his death, Jamsetji had envisioned and begun work on four major projects:
1.  Building a 5-star hotel (The Taj Hotel, completed during his lifetime) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:02">[00:08:02]</a>.
2.  Setting up a hydroelectric power plant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:55">[00:07:55]</a>.
3.  Establishing world-class educational institutions in India <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:57">[00:07:57]</a>.
4.  Setting up a steel plant <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:00">[00:08:00]</a>.

The latter three projects were continued and brought to fruition by his son, Dorabji Tata <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:17">[00:08:17]</a>.

## Dorabji Tata's Leadership

By 1910, Tata's steel plant was built and had begun production <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:23">[00:08:23]</a>. During World War I, Tata Steel became the largest steel supplier in the country, providing steel for British tanks, weapons, and railway tracks, leading a British politician to famously declare, "Tata Steel saved us" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:31">[00:08:31]</a>. The quality of their steel, which could withstand bomb impacts, further solidified Tata Steel's reputation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:53">[00:08:53]</a>. By 1914, the Tata Group had grown to encompass 14 different companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:08">[00:09:08]</a>.

## JRD Tata's Era and Post-Independence Challenges

Dorabji Tata led the group until 1938, when his distant cousin, Jahangir Ratan Tata (JRD Tata), took over <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:16">[00:09:16]</a>. JRD Tata, a licensed pilot, indulged his passion for flying by establishing India's first airline, Tata Air Lines, later renamed Air India <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:35">[00:09:35]</a>.

The year 1947 marked India's independence <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:58">[00:09:58]</a>. The prevailing socialist policies of Jawaharlal Nehru led to the nationalization of major businesses, including Tata Air Lines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:01">[00:10:01]</a>. Despite being heartbroken, JRD Tata, believing in business for [[impact_of_political_changes_on_tata_group | nation-building]], accepted the Nehru government's offer to lead Air India <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:30">[00:10:30]</a>. Under his leadership, Air India became one of the most prestigious airlines globally in the 1970s and 1980s <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:51">[00:10:51]</a>.

JRD Tata expanded the group into many other sectors:
*   In 1945, Tata Motors produced its first locomotive engines <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:03">[00:11:03]</a>.
*   In 1968, Tata Consultancy Services (TCS) was founded, providing electronic data processing, and today is India's second-largest employer after the Indian Railways <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:08">[00:11:08]</a>.
*   The group also ventured into cancer research and treatment, salt production, and electronics manufacturing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:21">[00:11:21]</a>.

During his 52-year tenure, the Tata group grew to include 95 companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:26">[00:11:26]</a>. When the Monopolies and Restrictive Trade Practices Act of 1969 aimed to prevent large companies from creating monopolies, JRD Tata adapted by diluting Tata Sons' ownership in subsidiaries, allowing the group to continue growing <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:36">[00:11:36]</a>. However, this strategy later proved problematic when some businesses, like the electronics arm Nelco, saw their market share plummet <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:04">[00:12:04]</a>.

## Ratan Tata's Transformation and Global Acquisitions

In 1971, Jamsetji Tata's grandson, [[ratan_tatas_leadership_and_global_acquisitions | Ratan Tata]], who had been working for the family business since 1962, was tasked with reviving Nelco <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:28">[00:12:28]</a>. He decided to cease radio manufacturing and invest in new technologies like satellite communications, successfully turning the business around within three years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:45">[00:12:45]</a>. Although Nelco was later shut down due to the Emergency declared in India <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:02">[00:13:02]</a>, Ratan Tata's capabilities were recognized by senior management <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:23">[00:13:23]</a>.

In 1991, Ratan Tata succeeded JRD Tata as the chairperson <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:26">[00:13:26]</a>, coinciding with India's liberalization under Finance Minister Dr. Manmohan Singh <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:35">[00:13:35]</a>. As India transitioned from a socialist to a capitalist model, Ratan Tata took a bold step, reversing JRD's strategy by increasing Tata Sons' ownership in all subsidiaries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:42">[00:13:42]</a>. This ensured that Tata Group companies could not be taken over by international competitors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:05">[00:14:05]</a>.

This strategy was immensely successful, allowing the Tata Group not only to survive but also to acquire foreign companies:
*   The Tetley Tea company in England was acquired for $12 billion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:16">[00:14:16]</a>.
*   Europe's steel giant, Corus, was acquired <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:24">[00:14:24]</a>.
*   The workers of Jaguar Land Rover, a UK car manufacturing company, specifically chose the Tata Group over 3-4 other prospective acquirers to take over their company <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:28">[00:14:28]</a>.

### Social Upliftment and Branding

Despite acquiring luxury brands, Tata Group maintained its focus on social upliftment. In 2003, Ratan Tata, observing a family struggling on a scooter in the rain, was inspired to launch the Nano Car five years later <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:54">[00:14:54]</a>. Marketed as the world's cheapest car at ₹100,000, its aim was to provide affordable car ownership to the lower middle class <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:10">[00:15:10]</a>. However, its marketing as "the most affordable car" was a flaw, as it negatively impacted consumer perception <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:24">[00:15:24]</a>.

The Tata Group has historically not focused heavily on branding, often establishing institutions and businesses without naming them after the Tata family <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:48">[00:15:48]</a>. Examples include the Indian Institute of Science <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:58">[00:15:58]</a>, the Empress Mill <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:00">[00:16:00]</a>, and the Taj Hotel <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:08">[00:16:08]</a>. Even the city of Jamshedpur, named after Jamsetji Tata, was named by the British Viceroy of India, not by the Tatas themselves <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:12">[00:16:12]</a>.
