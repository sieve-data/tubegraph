## Endocrinology

Endocrinology is consistently highlighted as the "king subject" in INI CET exams, often yielding a substantial number of questions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>. Topics frequently tested include:

*   **Pheochromocytoma**
    *   The preferred first investigation for pheochromocytoma is a 24-hour urine fractionated metanephrine test, known for its high sensitivity and specificity (both 98%) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:06:00">[03:06:00]</a>.
    *   If further confirmation is needed after the urine test, or if looking for 100% sensitivity, a plasma fractionated metanephrine test can be done <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:22:00">[03:22:00]</a>.
    *   Initial imaging is typically MRI abdomen, and for locating extra-adrenal sites or in cases where the mass isn't found, a Gallium Dotatate scan is preferred over MIBG <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:29:00">[03:29:00]</a>.
*   **Adrenal Medulla**
    *   Hypoglycemia is a primary stress response that stimulates catecholamine synthesis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:22:00">[03:22:00]</a>. Other physiological stimulants include standing, decreased intravascular volume, exercise, and smoking <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:22:00">[03:22:00]</a>.
*   **Hormone Receptor Matching**
    *   Insulin signaling involves tyrosine kinase receptors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:28:00">[03:28:00]</a>.
    *   Acetylcholine acts via ligand-gated ion channels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:28:00">[03:28:00]</a>.
    *   Prolactin acts through EP-JAK-STAT pathways <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:28:00">[03:28:00]</a>.
    *   Thyroxine acts on nuclear receptors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:28:00">[03:28:00]</a>.
*   **Autoimmune Polyendocrine Syndromes (APS/PGA)**
    *   APS type 1 (also known as autoimmune polyendocrinopathy with candidiasis and ectodermal dystrophy - APECED) is due to an auto-regulatory gene defect <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:49:00">[03:49:00]</a>.
    *   It typically starts very early in life, even infancy, and is highly associated with chronic mucocutaneous candidiasis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:52:00">[03:52:00]</a>.
    *   The three cardinal features are Graves' disease (hyperthyroidism), hypoparathyroidism (causing hypocalcemia), and Addison's disease <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:52:00">[03:52:00]</a>.
    *   Other significant manifestations include Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (around 23% association) and hypogonadism <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:52:00">[03:52:00]</a>.
*   **Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN) / MEON Syndromes**
    *   Questions on MEN and MEON syndromes, including Carney Complex, are common <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:30:00">[03:30:00]</a>.
    *   Carney Complex is famously associated with atrial myxoma (neoplasia part) and adrenal Cushing's (endocrine part), specifically primary pigmented nodular adrenal hyperplasia (or disease), which can present as lean or cyclical Cushing's <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:43:00">[03:43:00]</a>.
    *   MEN 2A and 2B are characterized by pheochromocytoma and medullary thyroid cancer (seen in nearly 100% of patients) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:54:00">[03:54:00]</a>.
    *   MEN 2A also involves parathyroid involvement, while MEN 2B presents with "4 Ms": mediated (or marbled) corneal nerve fibers, megacolon, marfanoid habitus, and mucosal ganglioneuromas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:54:00">[03:54:00]</a>.
*   **Prolactin**
    *   Physiology questions on prolactin are common, such as its role in causing hypogonadotropic hypogonadism through suppression of the axis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:37:00">[04:37:00]</a>.
*   **[[calcium_metabolism_and_its_significance_in_exams | Calcium Metabolism]]**
    *   Lab-related questions about calcium estimation are common <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:47:00">[04:47:00]</a>.
    *   The basic theory is that ionized calcium and H+ compete for binding to albumin <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:55:00">[04:55:00]</a>.
    *   Acidosis leads to hypercalcemia (more H+ binds to albumin, increasing free calcium) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:55:00">[04:55:00]</a>.
    *   Alkalosis leads to hypocalcemia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:55:00">[04:55:00]</a>.
    *   Practices like making a fist during blood collection, collecting after exercise, or tying a tourniquet too tightly can cause acidosis and alter calcium levels, thus are not recommended for calcium estimation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:55:00">[04:55:00]</a>.
*   **Diabetes**
    *   Insulin stimulates most bodily processes and enzymes, but it *inhibits* hormone-sensitive lipase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:08:00">[04:08:00]</a>.
    *   Hormone-sensitive lipase is a key player in lipolysis, leading to uncontrolled free fatty acids, especially in centripetal obesity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:20:00">[04:20:00]</a>. These free fatty acids contribute to gluconeogenesis and hepatic glucose production, promoting insulin resistance <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:20:00">[04:20:00]</a>.

## Nephrology

Nephrology is the third "prime specialty" for INI CET exams, often featuring a "handful of questions" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>. Questions often integrate with ABG analysis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:51:00">[02:51:00]</a>.

*   **Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis**
    *   [[metabolic_disorders_in_biochemistry_for_neet_pg_exam | Metabolic disorders in biochemistry for NEET PG exam]] frequently involve understanding anion gap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:33:00">[06:33:00]</a>.
    *   **High Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis (HAGMA)**: Occurs when unmeasured anions increase <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:57:00">[07:57:00]</a>.
        *   Common causes include:
            *   Keto acids: Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA), alcoholic ketoacidosis, starvation ketoacidosis, and euglycemic ketosis (e.g., due to SGLT2 inhibitors) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:50:00">[09:50:00]</a>.
            *   Lactic acid: Type A (due to hypoxia from conditions like ventilation-perfusion mismatch, sepsis, or shock) and Type B (due to liver failure, drugs like metformin, ethylene glycol, methanol, salicylates, or inborn errors of metabolism) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:24:00">[10:24:00]</a>.
            *   Uremic acid: Seen in Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) or Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) with decreased GFR <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:42:00">[11:42:00]</a>.
    *   **Normal Anion Gap Hyperchloremic Metabolic Acidosis (NAGMA)**: Occurs when bicarbonate loss is compensated by an increase in chloride <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:05:00">[08:05:00]</a>.
        *   Common causes include:
            *   Renal Tubular Acidosis (RTA): Type 1, Type 2, and Type 4 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:21:00">[12:21:00]</a>. RTA is typically associated with a positive urine anion gap <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:21:00">[12:21:00]</a>.
            *   Gastrointestinal (GI) bicarbonate losses: Diarrhea (e.g., in WDHA syndrome with VIP-secreting tumors), pancreatic leaks, enterocutaneous fistulas, or ureterosigmoidostomy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:21:00">[12:21:00]</a>.
*   **Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) Staging**
    *   The KDIGO classification for CKD staging (G1-G5 based on GFR) is frequently tested <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:10:00">[15:10:00]</a>.
        *   G1: GFR ≥ 90 mL/min (with evidence of kidney disease) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:10:00">[15:10:00]</a>
        *   G2: GFR 60-89 mL/min <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:10:00">[15:10:00]</a>
        *   G3a: GFR 45-59 mL/min <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:10:00">[15:10:00]</a>
        *   G3b: GFR 30-44 mL/min <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:10:00">[15:10:00]</a>
        *   G4: GFR 15-29 mL/min <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:10:00">[15:10:00]</a>
        *   G5: GFR < 15 mL/min (End-Stage Renal Disease, often requiring dialysis) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="15:10:00">[15:10:00]</a>
    *   Albuminuria staging (A1-A3 based on albumin-creatinine ratio) is also important <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:40:00">[16:40:00]</a>:
        *   A1: < 30 mg/g <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:40:00">[16:40:00]</a>
        *   A2: 30-300 mg/g <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:40:00">[16:40:00]</a>
        *   A3: > 300 mg/g <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="16:40:00">[16:40:00]</a>
    *   Clinical documentation typically combines GFR and albuminuria stages (e.g., CKD G5 A3) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="17:00:00">[17:00:00]</a>.
*   **Renal Transplant Rejection**
    *   An increase in creatinine post-transplant necessitates ruling out simple correctable issues (obstruction, venous thrombosis, dehydration, drugs) before suspecting rejection <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:10:00">[18:10:00]</a>.
    *   For cellular rejection, a >25% rise from baseline creatinine is an indication for biopsy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:38:00">[18:38:00]</a>.
    *   For humoral or antibody-mediated rejection (especially with donor-specific antibodies or ABO incompatibility), *any* rise in creatinine warrants a biopsy due to the urgency of treatment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="18:38:00">[18:38:00]</a>.
*   **Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA)**
    *   TMA is an umbrella term for conditions like Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP), Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS), preeclampsia/eclampsia/HELLP syndrome, malignant hypertension, and scleroderma renal crisis <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:02:00">[22:02:00]</a>.
    *   Common features of TMA include microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA) and thrombocytopenia, resulting from platelet plug formation in small vessels <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:08:00">[23:08:00]</a>.
    *   **HUS**: Primarily characterized by MAHA, thrombocytopenia, and renal failure, often due to endothelial injury from toxins like *E. coli O157:H7* or *Shigella dysenteriae* type 1 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:25:00">[23:25:00]</a>.
    *   **TTP**: Primarily characterized by MAHA, thrombocytopenia, and neurological symptoms, due to anti-ADAMTS13 antibodies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:38:00">[23:38:00]</a>. Renal involvement in TTP is rare <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:38:00">[23:38:00]</a>.
*   **Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) Physiology**
    *   GFR is determined by the net ultrafiltration pressure and the ultrafiltration coefficient (Kf) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:00:00">[27:00:00]</a>.
    *   Net Ultrafiltration Pressure = (Glomerular capillary hydrostatic pressure - Bowman's space hydrostatic pressure) - (Glomerular capillary oncotic pressure - Bowman's space oncotic pressure) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:00:00">[27:00:00]</a>.
    *   Factors that *decrease* GFR include increased Bowman's space hydrostatic pressure or increased glomerular capillary oncotic pressure <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="27:51:00">[27:51:00]</a>.
*   **Glomerular Hematuria**
    *   Glomerular hematuria is characterized by specific urine findings:
        *   ≥40% dysmorphic RBCs <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:21:00">[29:21:00]</a>.
        *   ≥5% acanthocytes (Mickey Mouse RBCs) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:21:00">[29:21:00]</a>.
        *   Presence of even a single RBC cast <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:21:00">[29:21:00]</a>.
    *   The urine is typically high-colored (brown, red, or cola-colored) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:42:00">[29:42:00]</a>.
    *   Dysuria is generally absent <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:51:00">[29:51:00]</a>.
    *   Crystals are not associated <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="29:57:00">[29:57:00]</a>.
    *   Edema is rare, depending on the underlying cause (e.g., IgA nephropathy vs. PSGN) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="30:00:00">[30:00:00]</a>.
