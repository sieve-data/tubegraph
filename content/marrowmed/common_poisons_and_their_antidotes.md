This article discusses various types of poisonings, their clinical manifestations, and respective treatments, including antidotes.

## Blast Injuries
Blast injuries result from explosions and are categorized based on the mechanism of injury. There are four types of blast injuries: primary, secondary, tertiary, and quaternary <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:04:00">[02:04:00]</a>.

The four products produced from an air blast are:
1.  **Blast Wave**: The initial pressure wave from the explosion spreading concentrically <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:00:00">[01:00:00]</a>.
2.  **Flying Missiles/Particles**: Objects or shrapnel propelled by the explosion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:09:00">[01:09:00]</a>. Terrorists may implant screws or nails into bombs to injure more people when they explode <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:13:00">[01:13:00]</a>.
3.  **Wind or Victim Displacement**: The victim is thrown away by the force of the blast <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:24:00">[01:24:00]</a>.
4.  **Miscellaneous**: Injuries from building collapse, fire, or other environmental factors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:34:00">[01:34:00]</a>.

### Types of Blast Injuries and Associated Effects
*   **Primary Blast Injury**: Caused by the blast wave <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:45:00">[01:45:00]</a>. These injuries predominantly affect air-filled organs like the ear, lung, and gastrointestinal (GI) tract <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:14:00">[02:14:00]</a>.
    *   The most common organ affected is the ear, potentially leading to tympanic membrane perforation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:32:00">[02:32:00]</a>.
    *   GI effects include colonic perforation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:39:00">[02:39:00]</a>.
    *   Lung injury is critical, causing diffuse lung damage, disruption of alveolar membranes, and Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)-like features, termed "blast lung" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:42:00">[02:42:00]</a>. Blast lung is considered the most fatal primary blast injury <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:02:00">[03:02:00]</a>.
*   **Secondary Blast Injury**: Caused by flying projectiles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:51:00">[01:51:00]</a>. These manifest as "martial stad," which are punctate lacerations, bruises, and abrasions <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:40:00">[00:40:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:13:00">[03:13:00]</a>. This injury is directional, indicating which body surface was facing the blast <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:31:00">[03:31:00]</a>. This is also the most common type of blast injury <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:27:00">[04:27:00]</a>.
*   **Tertiary Blast Injury**: Results from victim displacement, where the person is thrown against a stationary object, leading to fractures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="01:56:00">[01:56:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="03:54:00">[03:54:00]</a>.
*   **Quaternary Blast Injury**: Includes miscellaneous injuries like traumatic asphyxia from building collapse or burns from fire <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="02:00:00">[02:00:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="04:07:00">[04:07:00]</a>.

## [[Phosphorus Poisoning and Treatment | Phosphorus Poisoning]]
Phosphorus exists in two main forms: white phosphorus (also called yellow phosphorus) and red phosphorus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:37:37">[05:37:37]</a>. White phosphorus is highly toxic <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="05:46:00">[05:46:00]</a>.

### Clinical Features
*   **Chronic Poisoning**: Primarily seen with occupational exposure to phosphorus vapors <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:04:00">[06:04:00]</a>.
    *   Causes "phossy jaw," also known as "Lucifer jaw" or "glass jaw," characterized by osteomyelitis and osteonecrosis of the mandible <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:15:00">[06:15:00]</a>.
    *   Symptoms include jaw swelling, pain, and pus discharge from sinuses with a garlic odor, characteristic of white phosphorus <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="06:29:00">[06:29:00]</a>.
*   **Acute Poisoning**:
    *   **Stage 1**: Gastrointestinal symptoms, including "smoky stool syndrome," where fumes may emanate from stools <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:00:00">[07:00:00]</a>. Vomit and stools may glow, indicating luminous vomitus and stool <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:23:00">[07:23:00]</a>.
    *   **Stage 2**: Asymptomatic period, which can be misleading <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:35:00">[07:35:00]</a>.
    *   **Stage 3**: Can lead to multi-organ failure, predominantly hepatic failure, with bleeding tendencies and hepatic encephalopathy <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="07:43:00">[07:43:00]</a>.
*   **Skin Contact**: White phosphorus is lipid-soluble and can cause deep burns, leading to systemic toxicity if absorbed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:06:00">[08:06:00]</a>.

### Treatment (Historical/Not Recommended Now)
Historically, [[Phosphorus Poisoning and Treatment | copper sulfate]] solution was used topically on burns to combine with phosphorus, creating black discoloration for easier removal of particles <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:34:00">[08:34:00]</a>. For acute oral poisoning, gastric lavage with 2% copper sulfate solution was also employed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:06:00">[09:06:00]</a>.
*   **Note**: [[Phosphorus Poisoning and Treatment | Copper sulfate]] itself is a metallic poison and can cause toxicity, so these methods are **no longer recommended** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:09:00">[09:09:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="09:25:00">[09:25:00]</a>.
*   For bleeding tendencies in acute poisoning, Vitamin K can be administered <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="08:01:00">[08:01:00]</a>.

## [[Aluminium Phosphate Poisoning Diagnosis and Treatment | Aluminium Phosphate Poisoning]]
[[Aluminium Phosphate Poisoning Diagnosis and Treatment | Aluminium phosphate]] is a commonly encountered poison, often available as a "rat killer tablet" (e.g., Quick Force, Celphos, Al Force) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:28:00">[10:28:00]</a>. One tablet typically contains 3 grams of [[Aluminium Phosphate Poisoning Diagnosis and Treatment | aluminium phosphate]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:01:00">[11:01:00]</a>.

### Mechanism and Clinical Features
Upon ingestion, [[Aluminium Phosphate Poisoning Diagnosis and Treatment | aluminium phosphate]] reacts with hydrochloric acid in the stomach or moisture, liberating highly toxic phosphine gas <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:05:00">[11:05:00]</a>. Phosphine gas has a characteristic garlic odor <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:26:00">[11:26:00]</a>.

Phosphine is a protoplasmic poison that affects cells by inhibiting cytochrome oxidase, causing lipid peroxidation, and damaging mitochondria <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:33:00">[11:33:00]</a>.
*   **Common manifestations** include profound shock, metabolic acidosis, hypomagnesemia, and arrhythmias <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="11:50:00">[11:50:00]</a>.
*   It also affects the respiratory system, leading to diffuse lung damage and ARDS-like features <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:20:00">[12:20:00]</a>.

### Diagnosis and Treatment
*   **Diagnosis**: The "silver nitrate breath test" is a key diagnostic tool <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="12:44:00">[12:44:00]</a>. A silver nitrate filter paper held in front of the patient's mouth turns black when exposed to exhaled phosphine gas, confirming [[Aluminium Phosphate Poisoning Diagnosis and Treatment | aluminium phosphate]] poisoning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:10:00">[13:10:00]</a>.
*   **Treatment**: There is **no specific antidote** for [[Aluminium Phosphate Poisoning Diagnosis and Treatment | aluminium phosphate]] poisoning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:57:00">[13:57:00]</a>. Treatment focuses on stabilizing the patient and addressing complications <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="13:38:00">[13:38:00]</a>:
    *   Stabilize vitals and provide anti-shock measures (e.g., IV fluids) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:01:00">[14:01:00]</a>.
    *   Decontamination via gastric lavage can be performed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:47:00">[14:47:00]</a>.
    *   Correct hypomagnesemia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:08:00">[14:08:00]</a>.
    *   Manage ARDS with mechanical ventilation <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:12:00">[14:12:00]</a>.
    *   Treat arrhythmias with anti-arrhythmic measures <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="14:19:00">[14:19:00]</a>.
    *   Magnesium sulfate is often administered as part of the supportive treatment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="10:06:00">[10:06:00]</a>.

## Heat-Related Syndromes

### Heat Exhaustion
Heat exhaustion primarily results from the loss of sodium and water (water depletion and sodium depletion) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:41:00">[21:41:00]</a>.
*   **Thermoregulation**: The body's thermoregulatory mechanism (hypothalamus) remains **intact** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:53:00">[21:53:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:35:00">[24:35:00]</a>.
*   **Symptoms**: Cerebral hypoperfusion leads to giddiness, dizziness, syncope, and tachycardia <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:06:00">[22:06:00]</a>. Orthostatic hypotension may be present <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="25:03:00">[25:03:00]</a>.
*   **Skin**: Typically moist <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:24:00">[22:24:00]</a>.
*   **Temperature**: Core body temperature is usually less than 105째F (40.5째C) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="21:11:00">[21:11:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:33:00">[22:33:00]</a>.

### Heat Stroke
Heat stroke is a more severe condition where the body's thermoregulation mechanism is **impaired or gone** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="22:47:00">[22:47:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:42:00">[24:42:00]</a>.
*   **Temperature**: Core body temperature rises above 105째F (40.5째C) <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:02:00">[23:02:00]</a>.
*   **Symptoms**: Tachycardia, tachypnea, altered sensorium <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:09:00">[23:09:00]</a>.
*   **Skin**: Characteristically **dry** <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:15:00">[23:15:00]</a> <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="24:51:00">[24:51:00]</a>. Sweating is usually absent in classical heat stroke, though it can be present in exertional heat stroke <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:25:00">[23:25:00]</a>.
*   **Prognosis**: A fatal condition <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:46:00">[23:46:00]</a>.
*   **Autopsy Findings**: Increased rectal temperature at the time of death and "postmortem caloricity" (PMC), indicating high body temperature persisting after death <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="23:57:00">[23:57:00]</a>.
