The term "100 bagger" originates from Peter Lynch's book *One Up On Wall Street*, where he used the phrase "10 bagger" to describe stocks that had multiplied in value by 10 times <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:05">[00:00:05]</a>. In India, many companies have achieved "100 bagger" status, with ICICI Securities identifying 33 such companies, three of which (Pi Industries, KEI Industries, and Bajaj Finance) have become "1,000 baggers" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:11">[00:00:11]</a>.

This phenomenon is not limited to individual stocks; indices can also achieve "100 bagger" status <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:29">[00:00:29]</a>. For instance, the BSE Sensex, which began in 1979 at a base value of 100 points, became a 100 bagger in February 2006 and is currently a 700 bagger <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:31">[00:00:31]</a>. Mutual funds have also demonstrated 100x growth, with early investors in Franklin India Blue Chip, HDFC Tax Saver, and Nippon India Growth Fund experiencing [[characteristics_of_multibagger_stocks | multibagger]] returns <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:00:44">[00:00:44]</a>.

## Christopher Mayer's Study on 100 Baggers

The concept of a 100 bagger was further explored by Christopher Mayer in his book, which analyzed thousands of companies from 1962 to 2014 <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:21">[00:01:21]</a>. He narrowed his list to 365 companies that delivered a 100 times return on a $1 investment <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:31">[00:01:31]</a>. This list included well-known names such as Walmart, Coca-Cola, Boeing, and IBM, with Berkshire Hathaway topping the list with an 18,000 times return <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:39">[00:01:39]</a>.

Key findings from Mayer's study:
*   **Diversity**: The list of 100 Baggers was diverse, not limited to companies of the same type or from the same industries <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:01:53">[00:01:53]</a>.
*   **Starting Size**: These companies were small, but not extremely small, when they started, with a median market cap of about $500 million <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:00">[00:02:00]</a>.
*   **Timeframe**: The average time for a 100 bagger company to reach this status was 26 years, which translates to a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 19.4% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:08">[00:02:08]</a>. While 60 out of 365 companies reached 100x within 15 years, more than half achieved it between their 16th and 30th year <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:21">[00:02:21]</a>. This emphasizes that becoming a 100 bagger requires significant time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:34">[00:02:34]</a>.

The average stock holding period in the US has decreased from 5 years in the 1970s to just 10 months in 2022, highlighting a "patience gap" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:02:47">[00:02:47]</a>. One solution to this is adopting "coffee can investing," which involves building a portfolio of 15 good quality businesses and holding them for 20-30 years, expecting a few to become mega [[characteristics_of_multibagger_stocks | multi-baggers]] <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:00">[00:03:00]</a>.

## [[Growth Drivers for 100 Bagger Stocks | Growth Drivers for 100 Bagger Stocks]]: The Twin Engines

According to Christopher Mayer, two primary drivers contribute to a stock becoming a 100 bagger <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:34">[00:03:34]</a>:

1.  **Growth in Earnings**: Over the long run, a company's earnings and its stock price tend to move in the same direction <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:43">[00:03:43]</a>. A higher rate of earnings growth accelerates the 100 bagger journey <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:03:58">[00:03:58]</a>.
    *   A business growing earnings by 10% annually would take around 48 years to 100x its earnings and share price <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:04">[00:04:04]</a>.
    *   At 20% annual growth, it would take almost half the time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:13">[00:04:13]</a>.
    *   At 40% growth, it could achieve it in just 14 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:21">[00:04:21]</a>.
    *   Mayer emphasizes finding companies that are growing their earnings per share at a fast rate <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:24">[00:04:24]</a>.

2.  **Growth in Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Multiple**: Merely growing EPS is not enough; a growing P/E multiple is also required <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:35">[00:04:35]</a>. This reflects how investors perceive the value of a company or sector over time <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:04:49">[00:04:49]</a>.

    *   **Hindustan Unilever Limited (HUL) Example**: Over 15 years, HUL's sales grew by 9%, net profits by 11.3%, and EPS by 10.7% annually <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:21">[00:05:21]</a>. However, its share price grew significantly higher at about 17% <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:36">[00:05:36]</a>. This extra growth came from its P/E multiple re-rating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:48">[00:05:48]</a>. HUL's P/E historically ranged from 25-35, but from 2012 onwards, its valuation upgraded to a 45-55 P/E band, and then in 2018, it shifted to a 65-75 multiple <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:05:52">[00:05:52]</a>.
    *   **Reliance Industries Limited (RIL) Example**: RIL operated in a 12-17 P/E band for many years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:06:57">[00:06:57]</a>. When it aggressively entered the telecommunications space with Jio in September 2016, its stock underwent a re-rating <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:06">[00:07:06]</a>. Investors used Bharti Airtel's P/E ratio (25-40) as an anchor to revalue Reliance Industries' multiple <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:17">[00:07:17]</a>.

A combination of earnings growth and P/E ratio growth are the "twin engines" and the surest way to achieve a 100 bagger <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:28">[00:07:28]</a>.

## [[Christopher Mayers Six Characteristics for 100 Baggers | Christopher Mayer's Six Key Characteristics for 100 Baggers]]

To identify future 100 Baggers, Christopher Mayer outlines six essential characteristics:

1.  **Start as Small Companies**: 100 Baggers typically begin as smaller companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:48">[00:07:48]</a>. For a large company like HDFC Bank to become a 100 bagger from its current size, it would need to reach a market cap that is three times the current market cap of all listed companies in India, which is highly improbable <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:07:57">[00:07:57]</a>. It's more effective to target smaller companies, perhaps up to 10,000 crores in market cap, like catching Titan in 2001-2002 when it was valued at only 200 crores <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:08:15">[00:08:15]</a>. This aligns with the concept of [[understanding_microcap_stocks | understanding microcap stocks]].

2.  **Super Long Growth Runway**: A company needs a long growth runway, not just in market share, but also in developing new products, new markets, exports, and services to create new revenue sources <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:00">[00:09:00]</a>. Reliance Industries, for example, has diversified into petrochemicals, retail, media, textiles, e-commerce, and telecommunications, with its Jio platform offering multiple avenues like mobile telephony, broadband, satellite telephony, and 5G <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:17">[00:09:17]</a>.

3.  **Ability to Reinvest Profits**: 100 Baggers are companies that can reinvest their profits back into the business and generate a high rate of return for shareholders <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:09:51">[00:09:51]</a>. This "snowballing effect" is crucial <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:39">[00:10:39]</a>. Mayer suggests looking for companies that offer a Return on Equity (ROE) of at least 15% or more <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:47">[00:10:47]</a>. However, high ROC/ROE alone isn't enough if the business has matured and its growth trajectory has slowed <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:10:53">[00:10:53]</a>.

4.  **Strong Economic Moat**: For a company to survive and grow for 20-30 years, a strong economic moat is vital <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:17">[00:11:17]</a>. An economic moat is a structural characteristic that makes it difficult for competitors, allowing the business to generate high returns on capital over an extended period <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:29">[00:11:29]</a>.
    *   Coca-Cola's global brand recognition is a significant moat <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:11:45">[00:11:45]</a>.
    *   Other types of moats include intangibles (patents, intellectual property), switching costs, low-cost provider, toll moats, network effects (e.g., YouTube, WhatsApp), cultural moats, and data moats <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:12:17">[00:12:17]</a>.

5.  **Consistent Track Record**: Winners tend to keep on winning <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:00">[00:13:00]</a>. Building a 100 bagger portfolio from consistent performers is more reliable than investing in turnaround situations <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:07">[00:13:07]</a>. Mayer also advises considering the gross profit margin (GPM) of a business, as it indicates the additional value offered by the product/service beyond its cost <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:20">[00:13:20]</a>. Apple, with its consistent GPM of over 35%, is an example <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:39">[00:13:39]</a>. Consistency should be sought in sales growth, EPS growth, debt levels, and capacity expansion <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:13:47">[00:13:47]</a>.

6.  **Owner-Operator Companies**: Businesses managed and operated by their founders who are also large shareholders have better odds of becoming 100 Baggers <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:00">[00:14:00]</a>. Examples include Reliance Industries, Wipro, Dr. Reddy's, HCL Tech, and many Tata and Adani group companies <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:18">[00:14:18]</a>. These owners, having "skin in the game," tend to make better capital allocation decisions, are disciplined about costs, careful with debt, thoughtful about acquisitions, and focus more on cash flows than reported profits <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:32">[00:14:32]</a>. Studies globally show that promoter-led companies often outperform professionally managed ones <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:14:55">[00:14:55]</a>.

## The Crucial Seventh Characteristic: Patience

Beyond the six characteristics, the most crucial factor for achieving a 100 bagger is patience <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:11">[00:15:11]</a>. Being the proud owner of a 100 bagger stock demands persistent holding for 15, 20, or even 25 years <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:18">[00:15:18]</a>. In today's distracting environment, investors must resist the urge to tinker with their portfolios or chase "the next shiny object" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:30">[00:15:30]</a>. The magic word is "inaction" â€“ stay put <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:46">[00:15:46]</a>.

Patience also applies to finding the right investment opportunity <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:50">[00:15:50]</a>. Having a proper system of investing ensures you don't miss potential [[characteristics_of_multibagger_stocks | multi-baggers]] and don't prematurely sell excellent businesses <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:15:57">[00:15:57]</a>. Christopher Mayer advises investors to be a "reluctant seller" <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:12">[00:16:12]</a>. As Phil Fisher stated:
> "If the job has been done correctly when a common stock is purchased, that is you've researched and analyzed the business well, then the time to sell it is almost never." <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:17">[00:16:17]</a>

To succeed in stocks, one needs the vision to see it, the courage to buy it, and the patience to hold it <a class="yt-timestamp" data-t="00:16:32">[00:16:32]</a>.
